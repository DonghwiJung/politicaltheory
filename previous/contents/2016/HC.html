How to Consent

발표자: Japa Pallikkathayil (Professor of University of Pittsburgh)

일시: 2016년 5월 6일(금) 15시 ~ 18시

장소: 서울대학교 사회과학대학 (16동) 국제회의실 (349호)


초록:

Consenting is a familiar part of everyday life. One might consent to a friend’s use of one’s car, to a doctor’s touch, or to a lover’s embrace. In the absence of one’s consent, one’s rights typically prohibit these actions. But one’s consent overrides this prohibition. Consenting thus enables us to shape our interactions with others in significant ways. But despite how ubiquitous and important consenting is, theorists disagree about how exactly one consents. Does one consent by having a certain kind of mental state, by performing a certain kind of action, or through some combination of the two? In this paper, I defend the last of these options and consider the implications this view has for recent debates about consent to sexual interactions.


추천 자료:

  • Tom Dougherty, “Yes Means Yes: Consent as Communication,” Philosophy and Public Affairs 43 (3) (2015): 224-253.
  • Larry Alexander, “The Ontology of Consent,” Analytic Philosophy 55 (1) (2014): 102-113.
  • Lani Anne Remick, “Read Her Lips: An Argument for a Verbal Consent Standard in Rape,” University of Pennsylvania Law Review, 141 (3) (1993): 1103-1151.
