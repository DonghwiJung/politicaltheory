Why Does the State Have the Right to Control Immigration?

Speaker: Sarah Song (U.C. Berkeley School of Law & Department of Political Science)

Date: Thursday, October 6, 2016

Location: Building of College of Social Sciences (Building #16), International Conference Room (Room # 349)


Abstract:

Why, if at all, does the state have the right to control immigration? Contemporary political theorists and philosophers writing about immigration have advanced four types of answers based on 1) the value of distinctive cultures, 2) the right to private property, 3) the value of freedom of association, and 4) the right to be free from unwanted obligations. I discuss the limits of these justifications, propose some desiderata that a compelling theory of the state’s right to control immigration must meet, and advance an alternative justification based on the idea of collective self-determination.


Reference:

  • Joseph Carens, “Aliens and Citizens: The Case for Open Borders,” The Review of Politics 49 (1987).
  • Christopher Heath Wellman, “Immigration and Freedom of Association,” Ethics 119 (2008).
  • Arash Abizadeh, “Democratic Theory and Border Coercion: No Right to Unilaterally Control Your Own Borders,” Political Theory 36 (2008).
