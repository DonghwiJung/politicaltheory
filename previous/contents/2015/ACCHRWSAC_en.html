A Contemporary Conception of Human Rights, and Why it Should be Acceptable to Confucians

Speaker: Mathias Risse (Harvard University)

Date: Thursday, June 4, 2015

Location: Building of College of Social Sciences (Building #16), Room # 349


Abstract:

This paper was originally prepared for the conference on the effectiveness of human rights law in Chicago, October 2014. I begin by explaining why I do not think any results of that inquiry should deter us from keeping the human rights paradigm as a vision for the future of humanity. Next I introduce my conception of human rights as presented in Risse (2012) in terms of a philosophically intriguing difference between Martin Luther King, Jr. and Malcolm X. Then I introduce the notion of human rights as well as the notion of a conception of human rights more abstractly, go on to illuminate those notions and in particular introduce and explore the notion of an “orthodox” conception of human rights. My own conception of human rights as membership rights in the global order is developed from the idea of an orthodox conception. I sketch my conception, and conclude by explaining why it should be plausible specifically to Confucians. This should go a certain way in making human rights talk more acceptable in countries in East Asia, specially China, where human rights are more strongly resisted than in other parts of the world.
